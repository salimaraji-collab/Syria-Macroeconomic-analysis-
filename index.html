The Economy Brief

The recovery has matured into a normalization.

Eighteen months after the political transition, Syria's economy is no longer in the early stabilization phase. The currency has retraced from its mid-2025 peak, food prices are rising in dollar terms for the first time in this cycle, and ports are operating at scale. The single most important question is whether real wages can stay ahead of food inflation.

Prepared by Senior Economist Office Anchor April 2026 Comparison Year on year Status Briefing
In summary

What April 2026 tells us

The currency has retraced from its mid-2025 appreciation peak and settled around 12,300 to the dollar. Food prices have begun to rise across most of the basket, and now in dollar terms as well — meaning vendors are raising real prices, not just adjusting to a weaker currency.

Stabilization has matured into normalization — neither the dividend of mid-2025 nor a return to crisis.

Real wages have improved at the bottom of the income distribution but remain fragile, with the wage gain only marginally ahead of dollar food inflation. Ports are operationally reopened at scale, and remote-sensing indicators show economic activity above pre-transition levels and agricultural conditions recovering from a depressed 2025 base.

The single most consequential number for this briefing is the +12% year-on-year food basket inflation in dollar terms: it tells us inflation is now a domestic story, not a currency one.

Key findings

Seven readings of the post-transition economy

+27%
Food basket SYP, YoY

Food inflation has returned

The food basket excluding bread is up 27% in pounds and 12% in dollars year-on-year. The dollar increase is the analytically important one — it confirms genuine vendor-side inflation, not currency adjustment. Eleven of fourteen tracked items are up.

+26%
Real wage gain, YoY

Real wages have improved — modestly

In dollar terms, the only honest measure of real purchasing power, the unskilled monthly wage rose from $78 to $98 — a 26% real gain. The headline +42% in pounds overstates the improvement: most of that simply offset the currency depreciation.

+13%
Bakery bread, YoY

Bread is no longer the inflation story

Bakery bread rose only 13% year-on-year — modest. The 7× shock from the December 2024 subsidy reset is now in the rear-view mirror. Shop bread, never subsidised, actually fell 13%. The right framing is a one-time level shift on the staple, not ongoing bread inflation.

+13%
USD/SYP, YoY

The pound is retracing toward equilibrium

The currency depreciated 13% year-on-year, settling at 12,300 to the dollar. It remains 18% stronger than November 2024 — orderly retracement from the appreciation peak, not a return to pre-transition territory. Volatility is roughly five times pre-transition levels.

+379%
Port DWT, YoY

Ports are operating at scale

Vessel deadweight tonnage at Tartus and Latakia rose 379% year-on-year. Shipping companies are routing vessels at scale. One caveat: this measures vessel capacity at the port, not goods unloaded — customs data would be needed to convert this signal into firm cargo volumes.

+9%
Nightlights, post vs pre

Economic activity is rising

Satellite-derived nightlights radiance is 9% above the pre-transition baseline; the April-on-April reading is +3%. As an open-data, politically-neutral measure, this is the cleanest piece of independent evidence that the recovery is real.

+56%
NDVI, March YoY

Agricultural conditions are recovering — from a poor base

The vegetation index for March 2026 is 56% above March 2025. The headline appears strong but the 2025 figure was an unusually poor agricultural year. The 2026 reading is meaningful recovery but does not yet reach pre-transition trend.

Supporting evidence

The data behind the briefing

Figure 01
Food basket evolution, two indices
WFP market data, 73 markets, 13-item national basket. Two indices on the same chart: with bread and without bread. The gap between them is the December 2024 bread subsidy reset, a one-time level shift. The dashed blue line is the bread-excluded basket in dollar terms.
Analysis

The basket bottomed in mid-2025 at the FX trough and has climbed steadily since, in both pound and dollar terms. From April 2025 to April 2026 the basket excluding bread rose 27% in pounds and 12% in dollars. The dual-currency increase is the analytically important point: world food prices moved only modestly in this window, which means the dollar-basket increase is too large to be explained by global pass-through, and the pound-basket increase is too large to be explained by currency depreciation alone.

The implication is that the price increase is now driven by domestic factors — vendor margins, post-subsidy adjustments, weak supply-side response — rather than external pass-through. This is a meaningful diagnostic shift from the earlier post-transition period when nearly all food-price movement could be attributed to FX.

Figure 02
Item-level changes, April to April
Every item in the basket, plus shop bread for contrast. Year-on-year change in pounds.
Analysis

Breadth is the diagnostic point. Eleven of fourteen tracked items are up year-on-year. Tomatoes (+98%, partly seasonal) and potatoes (+45%) lead the increases; oil, meat, onions and lentils form a substantial second tier. Sugar and wheat flour are essentially flat. Only shop bread sits outside the inflation pattern.

When inflation is broad-based, substitution within the basket cannot offset the price impact. For households at the bottom of the income distribution, this is the most consequential signal in the data.

Figure 03
The pound on the parallel market
Monthly median of the unofficial USD/SYP rate. Three phases visible: sharp appreciation December 2024 to June 2025, gradual retracement through end-2025, steeper retracement in early 2026.
Analysis

Three distinct phases mark the post-transition currency cycle. Phase 1 (December 2024 to June 2025): sharp appreciation from 14,963 to 9,588 — a 36% strengthening, driven by confidence inflows, sanctions easing, and reduced regime-related capital flight. Phase 2 (June to December 2025): gradual retracement back to the 11,500–12,000 band as the import bill reasserted itself. Phase 3 (early 2026): steeper retracement to the current 12,300–13,000 range.

The rate has settled in a band well below the November 2024 peak (still 18% stronger) but materially weaker than the mid-2025 trough. Volatility is now around five times pre-transition levels — characteristic of a market in price discovery, not crisis.

Figure 04
The unskilled monthly wage, in dollars
Median across 73 markets, converted to dollars at the prevailing parallel rate, scaled to 22 working days per month. Showing wages in dollars strips out currency-driven nominal change and leaves the real purchasing-power signal.
Analysis

The real gain is small but genuine. Monthly wage in dollar terms rose from $78 in April 2025 to $98 in April 2026 — a 26% real gain. The headline +42% in pounds overstates the improvement: roughly 16 percentage points of that simply offsets the currency depreciation. The dollar line is much smoother than the pound line would be, because most of the apparent volatility is currency noise rather than real-wage volatility.

Through 2025 the dollar wage oscillated between $87 and $109; in 2026 it has settled around $100. The reconstruction-adjacent labour market is tightening, but the absolute gain at the bottom remains modest: roughly $20 per month in real terms. Set against the +12% dollar food inflation, real wage growth is only marginally ahead of price growth.

Figure 05
Economic activity, from orbit
Mean monthly radiance over Syria, NASA Black Marble. Nightlights are widely used as a satellite-derived, high-frequency proxy for urban economic activity, electrification, and population concentration. The signal is independent of any administrative reporting.
Analysis

The post-transition period sits visibly above the 2019–2024 plateau, with a particularly sharp peak in mid-2025 corresponding to the FX appreciation phase. The April 2026 reading is 3% above April 2025, and the full post-transition average is 9% above the pre-transition baseline. Because nightlights capture activity independently of official statistics, this is the cleanest piece of independent evidence that the recovery is real.

Figure 06
Agricultural conditions, vegetation index
Normalised Difference Vegetation Index from MODIS satellite data. Captures the agricultural production cycle: peaks in March–April reflect winter-crop greenery; troughs in summer reflect post-harvest dry conditions.
Analysis

Recovery from a depressed base. The March 2026 reading of 0.27 is a strong 56% above March 2025. The headline appears very positive, but it needs context: March 2024 was 0.31, meaning the March 2025 figure was an unusually poor agricultural year — likely the combined effect of drought and disruption to sowing operations during the transition itself.

The 2026 reading is therefore a meaningful recovery but not yet a return to pre-transition trend. Agricultural conditions are clearly improving, but the wheat-belt north-east and rain-fed areas have not fully normalised.

A methodological note The data measures vessel deadweight tonnage — the carrying capacity of ships arriving and departing. It is not a measure of cargo unloaded. A high figure means large ships are visiting Syrian ports; it does not directly indicate goods imported. For actual import volumes, customs data is needed.
Figure 07
Tartus and Latakia, monthly traffic
Total daily vessel deadweight tonnage at both Syrian commercial ports, aggregated to monthly. Incoming above the line, outgoing below.
Analysis

A structural shift. From an average of approximately 60,000 DWT per month through 2024 to 593,000 in April 2026 — roughly ten times the pre-transition baseline. On a year-on-year basis, incoming vessel capacity rose 379%. The pattern is consistent across the post-transition period rather than a single-month spike, suggesting a durable change in shipping-line behaviour.

Dry bulk dominates the increase, which is consistent with reconstruction inputs — cement, grains, fuel. The composition signal points clearly toward reconstruction-driven imports, but customs verification would be needed to state cargo volumes with the same confidence as port-call activity.

Sources

WFP Syria VAM (food prices, unskilled wage, parallel exchange rate, 73 markets). Tartus and Latakia port-call data, daily vessel deadweight tonnage, 2019 to April 2026. NASA VIIRS Black Marble (nightlights radiance). MODIS NDVI (vegetation index).

Notes on method

All comparisons are April 2026 versus April 2025 unless otherwise stated. Wages are converted to dollars at the prevailing parallel rate and scaled to 22 working days per month to leave the real purchasing-power signal. Vessel-traffic figures measure ship capacity at the port, not customs-confirmed cargo volumes.
